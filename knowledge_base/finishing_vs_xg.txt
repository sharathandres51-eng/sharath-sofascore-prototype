Expected Goals (xG) is a statistical metric that assigns a probability value (0 to 1) to each shot based on historical data, reflecting the likelihood that a shot taken from that position and in those circumstances results in a goal. An xG of 0.3 means that, on average, a shot of that quality is converted 30% of the time. A penalty has an xG of around 0.76; a tap-in from 3 metres is close to 0.95; a speculative long-range effort might be 0.03 or lower.

Comparing actual goals to xG reveals whether a team finished above or below expectation. A team that scores significantly more goals than their xG (overperforming xG) may be benefitting from exceptional finishing quality, or from luck — this is generally unsustainable over a full season but can absolutely decide individual matches. A team underperforming their xG either has poor finishing, is creating low-quality shots despite high volume, or is facing an exceptional goalkeeper. In tactical analysis, xG is most useful for judging the quality of chances created and conceded, rather than just the raw shot count. A team can lose a match while generating higher xG than their opponent, suggesting the result was not a fair reflection of the play.

Key terms: xG overperformance (goals > xG), xG underperformance (goals < xG), xGOT (Expected Goals on Target) — which considers only shots that required a save, making it a better measure of finishing quality and goalkeeper performance. A player who consistently generates high xGOT relative to their xG is placing their shots well, forcing difficult saves rather than missing the target.

Lionel Messi in the 2018/19 La Liga season was arguably the most extreme xG overperformer in history when adjusted for the quality and volume of chances he created — his ability to score from positions and angles that the model assigns low probability to reflects a qualitative superiority the metric cannot fully capture. This is important context when interpreting Barcelona's match data: their actual goals will often exceed their xG, particularly in games where Messi was the primary finisher.

The relationship between shots and xG also reveals tactical patterns. A team with many shots but low total xG is generating predominantly low-quality chances — long-range efforts, shots from poor angles, or shots under heavy pressure. This typically indicates a team that is struggling to create genuine opportunities and may be resorting to speculative attempts out of desperation. A team with fewer shots but higher total xG is generating cleaner, more central chances — a hallmark of effective build-up play and well-executed attacking patterns.

Shot location within the penalty area is the single biggest driver of xG value. Shots from the central zone within 10 metres of goal have the highest conversion rates. Headers from crosses into the six-yard box are high xG despite being harder to score than open-play shots. The type of assist also matters — a shot from a cut-back (ball played back from the byline into the box) tends to have higher xG than one from a whipped cross because the defender and goalkeeper are less able to organise.

When assessing fairness of a result in a single match, a difference of 0.5 xG or less is generally within the margin of variance and does not strongly indicate an unfair outcome. A difference of 1.0 xG or more is more meaningful — the team generating the higher xG had a substantially better claim to winning the match.
