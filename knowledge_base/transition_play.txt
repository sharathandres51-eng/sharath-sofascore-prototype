Transitions refer to the moments immediately after a team wins or loses possession — the switch from defence to attack (positive transition) or attack to defence (negative transition). These are often the most chaotic and dangerous phases of a match because both teams are temporarily out of their organised shapes.

Teams that excel in positive transitions can generate high-quality chances quickly before the defence recovers. In StatsBomb data, transition efficiency can be inferred by looking at the speed of sequences that lead to shots — fast transitions from defensive actions to shots within a small number of events suggest a team is effective in this phase. Negative transition quality is reflected in how quickly a team regains their defensive shape after losing the ball — teams with high pressure counts immediately after possession loss are well-drilled in their counter-pressing transitions.

The positive transition phase begins the moment a team wins the ball. In the first 2-3 seconds, the opponent is typically unorganised — some players are still in attacking positions, and the defensive shape has gaps. The team that has just won possession must make an immediate decision: play forward quickly to exploit the transition, or retain possession patiently to build an organised attack. Teams with pace and directness up front will nearly always choose the former; possession-based sides will depend on the situation.

The key variables in positive transition quality are: the speed of the first forward pass or carry, the number of players who can immediately support the ball carrier in the forward direction, and the ability of the forward players to hold their runs until the turnover is secured. A team with slow-starting forwards who need time to set up will not be able to exploit transitional moments even if the midfield wins the ball in a good position.

Negative transitions — the moments after losing possession — are increasingly viewed as among the most decisive in modern football. A team that transitions poorly from attack to defence will repeatedly be caught with players out of position, defenders exposed in 1v1 or 2v1 situations, and gaps between the defensive lines. Barcelona in 2018/19 mitigated this risk through their sophisticated counter-press: Messi, while not a high-volume presser in conventional terms, was expert at positioning himself to cut passing lanes immediately after a turnover, while Busquets dropped to protect the space in front of the defence. The question in any Barcelona match is whether this was effective — were they frequently exposed on the counter, and if so, did the counter-press prevent those moments from becoming chances?

The quality of a team's negative transition can often be read through the opponent's shot map: many high-xG chances from fast, direct sequences (few events between defensive action and shot) suggest the team is being exposed in transition. Conversely, if the opponent's shots are predominantly from set pieces or patient build-up, the team's defensive transition is working well.

Substitutions are a major lever for managing transitions. Bringing on a physically fresh midfielder in the 60th-70th minute can significantly improve both the pressing and the positive transition, as tired midfielders in the late stages of a match often fail to apply immediate pressure after turnovers or are too slow to make supporting runs in the forward transition. A manager who makes early attacking substitutions may be prioritising the positive transition; one who makes defensive substitutions is protecting the negative transition.

Set piece and dead ball situations are technically outside the transition framework but often arise from successful pressing moments — a forced error leads to a free kick, a heavy touch in a pressured situation leads to a corner. These moments represent the downstream value of pressing and transition work, and in a match where the goal count from open play is low, the set piece record may reveal which team's pressing was more productive.
