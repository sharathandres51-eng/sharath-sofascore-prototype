The half-spaces are the two vertical channels of the pitch that sit between the central lane and the wide lanes — roughly the areas between a fullback and a centre-back when the opposition is in a mid or low block. Exploiting the half-spaces is a central element of modern positional play and is closely associated with the tactical evolution pioneered at Barcelona and later spread through European football.

These zones are particularly dangerous because defending players must make difficult decisions — if the fullback steps in to close the half-space, it opens space wide for an overlap; if the centre-back steps across, it creates space centrally between the two defenders. Neither decision is clean, and a well-timed pass or dribble into the half-space from an advanced midfielder or inside forward forces one of these uncomfortable choices.

In StatsBomb event data, half-space exploitation can be seen through the average positions of attacking midfielders or inside forwards who drift into those channels, and through shot or pass origination coordinates in the 18-30 metre range from goal, offset from the centre. The classic half-space shot is the curling effort from the edge of the box toward the far post — a technique that Messi, Ribéry, Robben, and Salah have all used to devastating effect. Because these shots come from a central-ish position, they have above-average xG values relative to their distance from goal.

Lionel Messi's role in Barcelona's 4-3-3 (and its many variants) during 2018/19 was fundamentally a half-space role. Nominally a right winger, Messi would drift centrally and operate as a false nine or half-space attacker, receiving the ball in the right half-space and either driving at defenders, playing through-balls into the central forward run, or shooting. His average position data in StatsBomb should reflect this — sitting closer to the right half-space and centre than the touchline, unlike a traditional winger.

The left half-space at Barcelona was often occupied by Philippe Coutinho or Ivan Rakitić making late runs, while Jordi Alba's overlapping run on the left simultaneously stretched the opposition by creating a wide overload. This dual threat — Alba wide and an attacker in the left half-space — created constant dilemmas for opposition fullbacks and is a textbook example of how half-space play and wide overloads interact tactically.

Defensively, teams trying to deny the half-spaces typically use a mid-block with a compact 4-4-2 or 4-5-1 shape, intentionally pushing the play wide and accepting crosses rather than allowing turns in the half-space. When this works against Barcelona, it is often visible in the data as a higher proportion of crosses and wide play, with fewer incisive central passes or carries.

Half-space passes — particularly those played in behind or into the feet of a forward in the half-space — are some of the highest value offensive actions in football. They force rotation in the defensive backline, create shooting angles, and often precede cutback assists or one-two combinations that break down even well-organised defences. The number of passes and carries originating from or arriving in these zones is a strong indicator of whether a team's attacking pattern was effective.
